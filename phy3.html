Chapter 3: Dynamics üî®

The Study of Motion and its Causes (Forces)

Core Concepts üõ†Ô∏è

**Dynamics** is the branch of mechanics that studies the motion of objects and the **forces** that cause that motion.

1. Force, Inertia, and Momentum

  • **Force ($\vec{F}$):** An agent that tends to produce or stop motion, or change the direction or shape of a body. It is a **Vector** quantity. SI Unit: **Newton (N)**.
  • **Inertia:** The inherent property of a body to resist any change in its state of rest or uniform motion in a straight line. **Mass is the quantitative measure of Inertia.**
  • **Momentum ($\vec{p}$):** The quantity of motion contained in a body. It is the product of mass and velocity. It is a **Vector** quantity.
    $$\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$$
    SI Unit: $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m/s}$.

2. Force and Weight

  • **Weight ($\vec{W}$):** The gravitational force exerted by the Earth on a body. It is calculated by mass $\times$ acceleration due to gravity.
    $$\vec{W} = m\vec{g}$$
    Weight is measured in **Newtons (N)**, while Mass is measured in **kilograms (kg)**. Weight changes with location (e.g., Moon), but mass remains constant.

Newton's Laws of Motion

1. Newton's First Law of Motion (Law of Inertia)

A body continues its state of **rest** or **uniform motion** in a straight line unless acted upon by an external **net force**.

  • **Significance:** Defines **Inertia** and establishes the concept of an inertial reference frame.

2. Newton's Second Law of Motion

The rate of change of a body's **momentum** is directly proportional to the **net force** applied and takes place in the direction of the net force.

  • **Formula Derivation:** For a constant mass $m$, this simplifies to:
    $$\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$
    Where $\vec{F}$ is the net force, $m$ is mass, and $\vec{a}$ is acceleration.
  • **Definition of Newton:** **One Newton (1 N)** is the force required to produce an acceleration of $1 \text{ m/s}^2$ in a body of mass $1 \text{ kg}$.

3. Newton's Third Law of Motion

To every action, there is always an **equal and opposite reaction**.

  • **Key Points:** The action and reaction forces always act on **two different bodies**.
  • **Examples:** A rocket pushing gas downwards (action) and the gas pushing the rocket upwards (reaction). Walking (foot pushes ground, ground pushes foot forward). [Image of action and reaction forces in Newton's Third Law]

Short Questions üìù

  1. **Define Force and mention its SI unit.**
    Answer: Force is an external agent that changes or tends to change the state of rest or uniform motion of a body. Its SI unit is the **Newton (N)**.
  2. **What is Inertia?**
    Answer: Inertia is the natural tendency of a body to resist a change in its state of motion. A heavier object has greater inertia.
  3. **Differentiate between Mass and Weight.**
    Answer: **Mass** is the amount of matter in a body (constant everywhere), measured in $\text{kg}$. **Weight** is the force of gravity acting on the mass ($\vec{W} = m\vec{g}$), measured in $\text{N}$ (varies with $g$).
  4. **State the formula for momentum and define its terms.**
    Answer: The formula is $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$, where $\vec{p}$ is momentum, $m$ is mass, and $\vec{v}$ is velocity.
  5. **What is the concept of balanced forces?**
    Answer: Balanced forces are two or more forces acting on a body whose **vector sum (net force) is zero**. They do not cause acceleration, so the body remains at rest or in uniform velocity.

Long Questions üìö

  1. **State and explain Newton's First Law of Motion, providing a relevant example.**
    Answer: The First Law states that an object will remain at rest or continue moving at a constant velocity unless an external net force acts upon it. It is also known as the Law of Inertia. **Explanation:** It shows that objects naturally resist changes in motion. **Example:** When a bus suddenly brakes, the passengers continue to move forward due to their inertia, needing a force (the seatbelt or the seat in front) to change their state of motion.
  2. **Derive the relationship $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$ from Newton's Second Law of Motion.**
    Answer: Newton's Second Law states that $\vec{F} \propto \frac{\Delta \vec{p}}{t}$. Since momentum $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$, the change in momentum ($\Delta \vec{p}$) is $m(\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i)$. $$\vec{F} \propto \frac{m(\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i)}{t}$$ Since acceleration $\vec{a} = \frac{\vec{v}_f - \vec{v}_i}{t}$, we substitute $\vec{a}$: $$\vec{F} \propto m\vec{a}$$ By defining the unit of force (Newton) such that the constant of proportionality is 1, we get: $$\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$$
  3. **Explain Newton's Third Law of Motion with two daily life examples.**
    Answer: The Third Law states that for every action force, there is an equal and opposite reaction force. **Crucially, these forces do not cancel each other out because they act on different bodies.** **Example 1 (Swimming):** A swimmer pushes the water backward (action), and the water exerts an equal and opposite force, pushing the swimmer forward (reaction). **Example 2 (Recoil of a Gun):** When a bullet is fired, the gun exerts a forward force on the bullet (action), and the bullet exerts an equal and opposite backward force on the gun (recoil/reaction).

Numerical Problems üî¢

Practice applying the formula $\vec{F} = m\vec{a}$.

1. Calculate the acceleration produced by a force of $50 \text{ N}$ in a body of mass $10 \text{ kg}$.

Given: $F = 50 \text{ N}$, $m = 10 \text{ kg}$. Using $F = ma \Rightarrow a = \frac{F}{m}$: $$a = \frac{50 \text{ N}}{10 \text{ kg}} = 5 \text{ m/s}^2$$

2. Find the momentum of a truck having a mass of $2000 \text{ kg}$ moving with a velocity of $15 \text{ m/s}$.

Given: $m = 2000 \text{ kg}$, $v = 15 \text{ m/s}$. Using $\vec{p} = m\vec{v}$: $$\vec{p} = (2000 \text{ kg})(15 \text{ m/s}) = 30,000 \text{ kg}\cdot\text{m/s}$$

3. Calculate the weight ($\vec{W}$) of a body on Earth if its mass ($m$) is $5 \text{ kg}$ (assume $g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2$).

Given: $m = 5 \text{ kg}$, $g = 10 \text{ m/s}^2$. Using $\vec{W} = m\vec{g}$: $$\vec{W} = (5 \text{ kg})(10 \text{ m/s}^2) = 50 \text{ N}$$
