Understanding Torque, Center of Mass, and Equilibrium

Core Concepts üìå

While previous chapters dealt with linear motion, this chapter explores the motion caused by forces that results in **rotation**.

1. Parallel Forces

  • **Definition:** Forces that are parallel to each other.
  • **Types:** **Like Parallel Forces** (act in the same direction) and **Unlike Parallel Forces** (act in opposite directions).
  • **Resultant Force:** The single force that has the same effect as all the individual forces combined. Calculated by vector addition.

2. Torque (Moment of Force)

  • **Definition:** The **turning effect** produced by a force. It is also called the **Moment of Force**.
  • **Formula:** Torque ($\tau$) is the product of the force ($F$) and the moment arm ($L$).
    $$\tau = F \times L$$
  • **Moment Arm ($L$):** The perpendicular distance from the axis of rotation (pivot) to the line of action of the force.
  • **SI Unit:** Newton-meter ($\text{N}\cdot\text{m}$).
  • **Direction:** Conventionally, **Counter-Clockwise** torque is positive, and **Clockwise** torque is negative.

Center of Mass and Equilibrium

1. Center of Mass (CM)

A point in a body where an applied force causes only **linear acceleration** without producing any rotation. The entire mass of the body can be considered to be concentrated at this point.

2. Center of Gravity (CG)

A point in a body where the **weight** of the body appears to act. For objects on Earth, the Center of Gravity and Center of Mass are often considered the same.

3. Principle of Moments

For a body to be in **rotational equilibrium**, the sum of the **clockwise moments** acting on it must be equal to the sum of the **counter-clockwise moments** about the same axis.

4. Equilibrium

When a body is in a state of **balance**. It satisfies two conditions:

  • **First Condition (Translational Equilibrium):** The net force ($\Sigma F$) acting on the body must be zero. The body is either at rest or moving with uniform velocity. $$\sum \vec{F}_x = 0 \text{ and } \sum \vec{F}_y = 0$$
  • **Second Condition (Rotational Equilibrium):** The net torque ($\Sigma \tau$) acting on the body must be zero. The body is not rotating or is rotating with uniform angular velocity. $$\sum \tau = 0$$

5. States of Equilibrium

  • **Stable Equilibrium:** When slightly disturbed, the body tends to return to its original position (e.g., a brick lying flat). The CG is raised during disturbance.
  • **Unstable Equilibrium:** When slightly disturbed, the body topples over to a lower energy state (e.g., a cone balanced on its tip). The CG is lowered during disturbance.
  • **Neutral Equilibrium:** When disturbed, the body remains in the new position (e.g., a ball rolling on a flat surface). The CG remains at the same height.

Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs) ‚ùì

1. The turning effect of a force is called:

a) Inertia
b) Momentum
c) Torque
d) Force couple

Answer: c) Torque

2. The SI unit of Torque is:

a) Joule
b) Newton
c) $\text{Newton}\cdot\text{meter}$
d) $\text{Watt}$

Answer: c) $\text{Newton}\cdot\text{meter}$

3. For a body to be in complete equilibrium, the net force and net torque must both be:

a) Equal to unity
b) Maximum
c) Zero
d) Constant

Answer: c) Zero

4. The stability of a body is increased by:

a) Raising the Center of Gravity
b) Widening the base
c) Narrowing the base
d) Reducing the mass

Answer: b) Widening the base

Short Questions üìù

  1. **Define the Moment Arm.**
    Answer: The Moment Arm is the **perpendicular distance** from the axis of rotation (pivot) to the line of action of the force.
  2. **What is the Principle of Moments?**
    Answer: It states that for a body in rotational equilibrium, the sum of all clockwise moments acting on the body must be equal to the sum of all counter-clockwise moments.
  3. **State the two conditions for complete equilibrium.**
    Answer: 1) **First Condition (Translational):** Net force ($\sum \vec{F}$) must be zero. 2) **Second Condition (Rotational):** Net torque ($\sum \tau$) must be zero.
  4. **What is the difference between Center of Mass and Center of Gravity?**
    Answer: **Center of Mass** is the point where an applied force causes linear motion only. **Center of Gravity** is the point where the weight of the body acts. They are usually the same near the Earth's surface.
  5. **How can the stability of a body be increased?**
    Answer: Stability can be increased by **lowering the Center of Gravity** and/or **increasing the width of the base** of the object.

Long Questions üìö

  1. **Define Torque. Explain the factors on which the turning effect of a force depends.**
    Answer: **Torque** is the turning effect of a force about an axis. It depends on two factors: **1) Magnitude of the Force ($F$):** A larger force produces greater torque. **2) Length of the Moment Arm ($L$):** A longer perpendicular distance from the pivot to the force (longer moment arm) produces a greater torque ($\tau = F \times L$). This is why it's easier to open a door by pushing it at the handle rather than near the hinge.
  2. **Explain the three states of equilibrium with examples of each.**
    Answer: **Stable:** Body returns to its original position when disturbed (e.g., a book lying flat). **Unstable:** Body moves to a new, lower energy position when disturbed (e.g., a pencil balanced on its tip). **Neutral:** Body remains in the new position when disturbed, with its CG staying at the same height (e.g., a billiard ball on a table). [Image of the three states of equilibrium: stable, unstable, and neutral]
  3. **Derive the relationship for the resultant of two unlike parallel forces acting on a body.**
    Answer: The resultant force ($R$) of two unlike parallel forces ($F_1$ and $F_2$, where $F_1 > F_2$) is their difference: $R = F_1 - F_2$. The direction of $R$ is the same as the larger force ($F_1$). The point of action of $R$ is found by the condition of rotational equilibrium (Principle of Moments), such that $R \times r = F_1 \times r_1 + F_2 \times r_2$, where $r_1$ and $r_2$ are distances from the pivot. More simply, the resultant acts outside $F_1$ and $F_2$, closer to the larger force.

Numerical Problems üî¢

Practice calculating Torque and using the Principle of Moments.

1. A force of $20 \text{ N}$ acts perpendicularly on a door at a distance of $0.8 \text{ m}$ from the hinges. Calculate the torque produced.

Given: $F = 20 \text{ N}$, $L = 0.8 \text{ m}$. Using $\tau = F \times L$: $$\tau = (20 \text{ N}) \times (0.8 \text{ m}) = 16 \text{ N}\cdot\text{m}$$

2. A meter rod is balanced at the $50 \text{ cm}$ mark. If a weight of $5 \text{ N}$ is placed at the $10 \text{ cm}$ mark, where must a weight of $20 \text{ N}$ be placed to keep it balanced?

Pivot is at $50 \text{ cm}$. $F_1 = 5 \text{ N}$ (Counter-clockwise), $L_1 = (50 - 10) \text{ cm} = 40 \text{ cm}$. $F_2 = 20 \text{ N}$ (Clockwise), $L_2 = x \text{ cm}$ (distance from $50 \text{ cm}$ mark). Principle of Moments: $\tau_{\text{CCW}} = \tau_{\text{CW}}$ $$F_1 L_1 = F_2 L_2$$ $$(5 \text{ N})(40 \text{ cm}) = (20 \text{ N}) L_2$$ $$200 = 20 L_2 \Rightarrow L_2 = 10 \text{ cm}$$ The $20 \text{ N}$ weight must be placed $10 \text{ cm}$ from the pivot, at the $(50 + 10) \text{ cm} = 60 \text{ cm}$ mark.
