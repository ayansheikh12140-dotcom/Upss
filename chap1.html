Chapter 1: Fundamentals of Chemistry

Comprehensive Notes, Questions, and Practice for Sindh Textbook Board

Chapter Notes

Chemistry is the branch of science that deals with the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter. This chapter introduces the basic concepts of chemistry, including matter, elements, compounds, mixtures, and the scientific method.

What is Matter?

Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space. It exists in three states: solid, liquid, and gas.

  • Solid: Definite shape and volume (e.g., ice).
  • Liquid: Definite volume but no definite shape (e.g., water).
  • Gas: No definite shape or volume (e.g., air).

Elements, Compounds, and Mixtures

Substances are classified into elements, compounds, and mixtures.

  • Elements: Pure substances that cannot be broken down into simpler substances (e.g., hydrogen, oxygen).
  • Compounds: Substances formed by chemical combination of two or more elements (e.g., water - H₂O).
  • Mixtures: Combinations of substances that can be separated by physical means (e.g., salt in water).

Physical and Chemical Changes

Changes in matter can be physical or chemical.

  • Physical Change: No new substance is formed (e.g., melting ice).
  • Chemical Change: New substances are formed (e.g., burning paper).

The Scientific Method

The scientific method involves observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and conclusion to understand natural phenomena.

Laws of Chemical Combination

Key laws include:

  • Law of Conservation of Mass: Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
  • Law of Definite Proportions: A compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass.

Multiple Choice Questions (MCQs)

1. Which of the following is not a state of matter?

a) Solid
b) Liquid
c) Gas
d) Plasma

Answer: d) Plasma (Note: Plasma is considered a fourth state, but in basic chemistry, options a-c are standard.)

2. What is the chemical formula for water?

a) H₂
b) O₂
c) H₂O
d) CO₂

Answer: c) H₂O

3. Which change is physical?

a) Burning of paper
b) Melting of ice
c) Rusting of iron
d) Cooking of food

Answer: b) Melting of ice

4. An example of a mixture is:

a) Water
b) Salt
c) Air
d) Gold

Answer: c) Air

5. The law that states mass is conserved in a chemical reaction is:

a) Law of Definite Proportions
b) Law of Conservation of Mass
c) Law of Multiple Proportions
d) Avogadro's Law

Answer: b) Law of Conservation of Mass

Short Questions

  1. Define chemistry.
    Answer: Chemistry is the branch of science that studies the composition, structure, properties, and changes of matter.
  2. What is matter?
    Answer: Matter is anything that has mass and occupies space.
  3. Name the three states of matter.
    Answer: Solid, liquid, and gas.
  4. Differentiate between elements and compounds.
    Answer: Elements are pure substances that cannot be broken down, while compounds are formed by chemical combination of elements.
  5. What is a mixture?
    Answer: A mixture is a combination of substances that can be separated by physical means.
  6. Give an example of a physical change.
    Answer: Melting of ice.
  7. Give an example of a chemical change.
    Answer: Burning of paper.
  8. What is the scientific method?
    Answer: It is a systematic approach involving observation, hypothesis, experimentation, and conclusion.
  9. State the Law of Conservation of Mass.
    Answer: Mass is neither created nor destroyed in a chemical reaction.
  10. State the Law of Definite Proportions.
    Answer: A compound always contains the same elements in the same proportion by mass.

Long Questions

  1. Explain the three states of matter with examples.
    Answer: Solids have definite shape and volume (e.g., ice). Liquids have definite volume but no definite shape (e.g., water). Gases have no definite shape or volume (e.g., air).
  2. Differentiate between physical and chemical changes with examples.
    Answer: Physical changes do not form new substances (e.g., melting ice), while chemical changes do (e.g., burning paper).
  3. Describe the classification of substances into elements, compounds, and mixtures.
    Answer: Elements are pure and indivisible (e.g., oxygen). Compounds are combinations of elements (e.g., water). Mixtures are physical combinations (e.g., salt solution).
  4. Explain the scientific method with steps.
    Answer: Steps include: observation, forming a hypothesis, conducting experiments, analyzing data, and drawing conclusions.
  5. Discuss the Laws of Chemical Combination.
    Answer: The Law of Conservation of Mass states mass is conserved. The Law of Definite Proportions states compounds have fixed proportions.

Numerical Problems

Note: Chapter 1 may not have many numericals, but here are some related practice problems.

  1. Calculate the mass of oxygen in 18g of water (H₂O, atomic masses: H=1, O=16).
    Answer: Water is H₂O, so 2H + O = 18g. Mass of O = 16g.
  2. In a reaction, 10g of reactant produces 8g of product. What is the mass of the other product? (Law of Conservation)
    Answer: Total mass conserved, so other product is 2g.
  3. A compound has 2g hydrogen and 16g oxygen. Find the ratio.
    Answer: Ratio H:O = 2:16 or 1:8.
