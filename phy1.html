Comprehensive Study of Units, Systems, and Measuring Instruments

Chapter Notes üî¨

This chapter lays the foundation for all physics studies by defining what can be **measured** and how it is quantified using standard **units**.

1. Physical Quantities

A **Physical Quantity** is any measurable quantity in physics. It consists of two parts: a **numerical magnitude** and a **unit**.

  • **Example:** 5 kilograms (5 is the magnitude, kilogram is the unit).
  • **Types:** **Base Quantities** and **Derived Quantities**.

These are the seven fundamental quantities that form the basis for all other measurements. The international system of units is the **SI (Syst√®me International d'Unit√©s)**.

3. Derived Quantities and Units

These are quantities expressed as a combination or product/quotient of two or more base quantities.

  • **Area:** Length $\times$ Length (Unit: $\text{m}^2$)
  • **Volume:** Length $\times$ Length $\times$ Length (Unit: $\text{m}^3$)
  • **Velocity:** Displacement / Time (Unit: $\text{m}/\text{s}$)
  • **Force:** Mass $\times$ Acceleration (Unit: $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}/\text{s}^2$ or Newton, N)

4. Scientific Notation and Prefixes

Used to express very large or very small numbers in a manageable format, using powers of ten ($a \times 10^n$, where $1 \le a < 10$).

  • **Example:** Speed of light $= 300,000,000 \text{ m/s} = 3.0 \times 10^8 \text{ m/s}$.
  • **Prefixes:** Multiples and sub-multiples of base units (e.g., kilo, milli, micro).

5. Measuring Instruments

Tools used to ensure accurate measurement:

  • **Meter Rule:** Measures length, usually up to $1 \text{ mm}$ (least count).
  • **Vernier Calipers:** Measures length with greater precision (Least Count typically $0.1 \text{ mm}$ or $0.01 \text{ cm}$).
  • **Screw Gauge (Micrometer):** Measures very small lengths, such as wire diameter (Least Count typically $0.01 \text{ mm}$ or $0.001 \text{ cm}$).
  • **Stop Watch:** Measures time interval.
  • **Physical Balance:** Measures mass.

Short Questions üìù

  1. **Define a Physical Quantity.**
    Answer: A measurable characteristic of an object or system (e.g., length, mass, time) that consists of a numerical magnitude and a unit.
  2. **Differentiate between Base and Derived Quantities.**
    Answer: **Base Quantities** are fundamental and independent (e.g., length, mass). **Derived Quantities** are combinations of base quantities (e.g., area, speed, force).
  3. **Write the SI units of Force and Density.**
    Answer: SI unit of Force is Newton ($\text{N}$) or $\text{kg}\cdot\text{m}/\text{s}^2$. SI unit of Density is $\text{kg}/\text{m}^3$.
  4. **What is scientific notation? Give an example.**
    Answer: A way of writing very large or very small numbers using powers of ten. Example: $6,000,000 \text{ m} = 6.0 \times 10^6 \text{ m}$.
  5. **What is the function and least count of a Screw Gauge?**
    Answer: The Screw Gauge is used to measure very small lengths, such as the diameter of a thin wire. Its least count is typically $0.01 \text{ mm}$.

Long Questions üìö

  1. **Explain the necessity of the SI system of units in science and trade.**
    Answer: The SI system is necessary because it provides a universal and consistent set of units. This eliminates confusion and errors that arise from different countries using different systems (like FPS, CGS), ensuring that scientific data and international trade measurements are understood and replicated uniformly across the globe.
  2. **Explain the working and use of Vernier Calipers.**
    Answer: Vernier Calipers are used to measure length, depth, and internal/external diameters more precisely than a meter rule. The working principle involves a **Vernier scale** sliding over a **Main scale**. The least count is calculated by dividing the smallest main scale division by the number of Vernier scale divisions. Readings are taken by adding the main scale reading to the coincident Vernier scale reading (after multiplying by the least count). [Image of Vernier Caliper]
  3. **What are the standard SI Base Units? Define any three of them.**
    Answer: The seven standard SI Base Units are meter, kilogram, second, ampere, kelvin, mole, and candela. **Meter:** The length of the path traveled by light in vacuum during a time interval of $1/299,792,458$ of a second. **Kilogram:** The unit of mass. **Second:** The duration of $9,192,631,770$ periods of the radiation corresponding to the transition between the two hyperfine levels of the ground state of the Caesium-133 atom.

Numerical Problems üî¢

Practice these conversions and calculations.

1. Convert $5000 \text{ g}$ into kilograms ($\text{kg}$).

Solution: Since $1 \text{ kg} = 1000 \text{ g}$, $5000 \text{ g} = 5000/1000 \text{ kg} = 5 \text{ kg}$.

2. Express $0.000007 \text{ seconds}$ in scientific notation.

Solution: $7.0 \times 10^{-6} \text{ s}$.

3. Convert $15 \text{ mm}$ to meters ($\text{m}$).

Solution: Since $1 \text{ m} = 1000 \text{ mm}$, $15 \text{ mm} = 15/1000 \text{ m} = 0.015 \text{ m}$.

4. If the velocity is $25 \text{ m/s}$ and time is $10 \text{ s}$, calculate the distance. (Formula: Distance = Velocity $\times$ Time)

Solution: Distance $= (25 \text{ m/s}) \times (10 \text{ s}) = 250 \text{ m}$.
